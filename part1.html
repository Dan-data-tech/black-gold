Part One · The Basics

The Fuel That
Built an Empire

You hear about oil and gas every day — in the news, in prices at the pump, in politics. But what actually are they? And why does the Gulf matter so much?

6 min read · Energy & Geopolitics · GCC Series

Six countries in the Middle East — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Qatar, Kuwait, Oman, and Bahrain — together form a group called the GCC (Gulf Cooperation Council). These six countries sit on top of some of the biggest oil and gas reserves on the planet.

To understand why that's such a big deal, let's start from the beginning.

What are fossil fuels?

Fossil fuels are natural substances found deep underground. They formed over millions of years from the remains of plants and animals that got buried and compressed over time. Today, we dig them up and burn them to produce energy.

There are three main types: oil, natural gas, and coal. The GCC's story revolves almost entirely around the first two.

Crude oil — what is it?

Think of crude oil as raw, unprocessed oil — exactly as it comes out of the ground. It's a thick, dark liquid and on its own, you can't do much with it. But once it goes through a refinery (a facility that processes and separates it), it turns into things we use every single day:

Gasoline (بنزين)
What you put in your car at the petrol station
Diesel (مازوت)
Trucks, generators — and heating in Lebanon and the region
Jet fuel (كيروسين)
Every plane in the sky runs on this
Plastics & chemicals
Your water bottle, your phone case, your medicine packaging

When you hear "crude oil supply," it simply means: how much raw oil is available in the world market right now — from all the countries producing and selling it.

Natural gas — what is it?

Natural gas is exactly what it sounds like — a gas that comes from underground. It's often found near oil fields, or in its own separate deposits. We measure it in cubic meters or cubic feet.

It's used to generate electricity, heat homes, run factories, and make fertilizers. One important thing to know: natural gas can be super-cooled into a liquid form called LNG (Liquefied Natural Gas). This makes it possible to load onto ships and export it anywhere in the world — which is exactly how Qatar became one of the most powerful energy exporters on the planet.

Who buys all this energy?

This is where things get interesting. Countries all over the world depend on GCC oil and gas to keep their lights on, their factories running, and their cars moving. The more a country depends on Gulf energy, the more it has to care about what happens there.

"When something goes wrong in the Gulf, the whole world feels it — at the pump, in electricity bills, and in the economy."

Japan gets nearly 90% of its oil from the GCC. South Korea gets 70–80%. Even big powers like China and India — with huge economies and populations — rely heavily on Gulf oil to keep going. Europe mostly buys natural gas, and that dependence grew even more after 2022 when Europe stopped buying Russian gas.

The bottom line? The GCC doesn't just sell fuel. It sells the world the ability to function.
